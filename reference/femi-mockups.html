Clinical reference · Cardiology

Myocardial infarction

A heart attack happens when blood flow to part of the heart muscle is suddenly cut off — usually by a blood clot in a coronary artery. The longer the muscle goes without oxygen, the more damage occurs. Minutes matter.

How it presents

The classic picture is a middle-aged or older patient describing sudden, crushing chest pain in the center of the chest that may radiate down the left arm or up into the jaw. Many describe a heavy weight pressing on them. The pain typically doesn't change with position, breathing, or rest.

Look for accompanying symptoms — nausea, sweating, shortness of breath, and a sense of impending doom. These are common but not universal. Women, older adults, and people with diabetes often present with much subtler symptoms, which is part of why so many heart attacks are missed.

"It feels like a weight on my chest." — A patient's own description is often more useful than any single vital sign.

Initial workup

The two most important early steps are an electrocardiogram within ten minutes of arrival, and a serum troponin level. ST-segment elevation on the ECG suggests a STEMI and triggers emergent reperfusion. A non-ST elevation MI is identified by elevated troponin without that telltale ST elevation.

  • 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes
  • Troponin, repeated at intervals
  • Chest X-ray to rule out other causes
  • Basic metabolic panel, CBC, coagulation studies

Immediate management

Time is muscle. Standard initial treatment includes aspirin, oxygen if hypoxic, nitroglycerin for ongoing pain, and pain control with morphine if needed. For a STEMI, emergent percutaneous coronary intervention is the gold standard — ideally within 90 minutes of first medical contact.
